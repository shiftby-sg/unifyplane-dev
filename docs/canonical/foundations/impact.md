## Impact

Impact is about what a change, drift, or runtime difference may affect next.

If Proof helps show what is true now, and Drift helps show what is separating over time, Impact helps explain where that movement may spread and why it matters.

This matters because many organizations do not fail only because something changed.
They fail because the downstream consequence of that change is understood too late.

A change may look small at first.
A drift may look local.
A runtime difference may seem contained.

But what matters next is:

* what else is affected
* how far that effect may spread
* which assumptions no longer hold
* what now requires attention
* how serious the consequence may become

That is the role of Impact.

---

## Why Impact Matters

Organizations often recognize a problem only after the consequence has already moved beyond the original change.

They may know that something changed.
They may even know that some drift is visible.
But they still may not know:

* what other services, repos, controls, or processes are touched
* whether the effect is local or widening
* whether the issue is mostly operational, structural, security-relevant, or business-relevant
* what needs revalidation first
* which consequence matters most now

That is why Impact matters.

It helps move from:

* “something changed”
to
* “this is what that change may now affect”

Without enough impact visibility, change stays too narrow, drift stays too local, and response often comes too late.

---

## Impact Is Not Only Damage

Impact should not be understood only as harm.

Impact is broader than damage.

It can include:

* affected dependencies
* affected runtime paths
* affected controls
* affected tests
* affected evidence expectations
* affected downstream systems or teams
* affected trust boundaries
* affected operating assumptions

Some impact is minor and manageable.
Some impact widens into a serious consequence.

The important thing is not to assume all change is severe.
The important thing is to understand where consequence may travel and what now matters most.

---

## Impact in Plain Language

Impact is about questions like:

* What else is touched by this change?
* What else may need to be checked now?
* Where could this drift spread next?
* Which downstream assumptions are no longer safe?
* Is this a small local effect or something wider?

That is why Impact is closely related to both Change and Drift.

A system can show proof of what happened now and still leave people uncertain about what that means next.

Impact is what begins to answer that second question.

## What UnifyPlane Means by Impact

In UnifyPlane, Impact is the foundation concerned with downstream consequence visibility.

It is about moving beyond point-in-time correctness and beyond recognition of drift into a clearer understanding of:

* what else may be affected
* how consequence may propagate
* what now needs reassessment
* where severity may be growing

That means Impact is not only about recording a change after the fact.
It is about helping make consequence more visible before it spreads too far.

A mature Impact capability would help answer:

* what dependencies are touched?
* what controls or boundaries may now need review?
* what evidence expectations may no longer hold?
* what downstream runtime paths may be affected?
* which consequence matters first?
* how far could this spread if left unaddressed?

---

## Impact Depends on the Other Foundations

Impact should be read together with the other foundations.

* **Proof** helps show what is true now
* **Drift** helps show what is separating over time
* **Change** helps show what moved and what now needs attention
* **Evidence** helps support what can actually be shown
* **Impact** helps explain what that movement may affect next

This means Impact depends on enough clarity in the earlier layers.

Without enough Proof, impact becomes guesswork.  
Without enough Drift visibility, impact becomes incomplete.  
Without enough Evidence, impact becomes too speculative.

So Impact does not replace the earlier foundations.
It builds on them.

## Impact Across the Lifecycle

Impact should not be treated as only a post-incident concept.

It can matter across multiple phases.

### 1. Design and intent phase

At this stage, impact questions may include:

* what else now needs to change to stay aligned?
* what assumptions no longer hold?
* what downstream design or test surfaces may be touched?

### 2. Development and integration phase

At this stage, impact questions may include:

* what dependencies may now be affected?
* what integrations may need reassessment?
* what tests or constraints may no longer be sufficient?

### 3. Release and readiness phase

At this stage, impact questions may include:

* what is the likely blast radius if this proceeds?
* what still needs validation before release?
* what downstream environments, controls, or consumers may be affected?

### 4. Runtime and operational phase

At this stage, impact questions may include:

* what changed in live behavior?
* what downstream services, processes, or trust assumptions may now be affected?
* what needs reassessment immediately?
* how serious could this become if it continues?

That is why Impact should be understood as a lifecycle concern, not only a late operational one.

## How Impact Appears in the Current State

Today, Impact in UnifyPlane is still early.

It is not yet a strong fully realized outward capability in the same way that Proof and Drift already are.

### What is already visible

The current system already provides some early foundations for impact reasoning, especially through:

* structural change visibility
* contract and repo inspection surfaces
* runtime conformance differences
* evidence-backed runtime closure
* early drift visibility across declared and runtime surfaces

These do not yet provide mature end-to-end consequence reasoning, but they do help expose the places where later impact assessment could begin.

### What is not yet mature

The current system does not yet clearly provide strong outward capability for:

* broad dependency-aware consequence visibility
* end-to-end blast-radius understanding
* mature severity reasoning
* broad lifecycle-wide impact assessment
* strong downstream organizational consequence interpretation

So today, Impact should be understood as a legitimate and grounded direction, but still an earlier one.

The broader direction of Impact in UnifyPlane includes stronger capability around:

* dependency-aware consequence visibility
* richer post-runtime interpretation
* clearer end-to-end impact assessment
* stronger reasoning about downstream effect
* better visibility into what now requires reassessment
* future consequence reasoning across conventional and AI-enabled execution surfaces

---

## What Impact Does Not Mean

Impact does not mean:

* every change is severe
* every drift signal becomes a major consequence
* the system can already predict every downstream effect
* current evidence is already enough for full consequence reasoning in every case

Impact is not perfect foresight.

It is the effort to make downstream consequence more visible, more explainable, and less surprising than it would otherwise be.

Today, Impact is one of the less mature foundations in UnifyPlane.
