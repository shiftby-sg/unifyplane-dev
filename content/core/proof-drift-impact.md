## Proof, Drift, and Impact

Proof, Drift, and Impact are three practical ways to understand what UnifyPlane is trying to make possible over time.

They are not marketing labels.
They are not separate products.
And they should not be treated as three unrelated ideas.

They are three different ways of looking at the same problem:

**The real problem is not just complexity. Over time, intended change, implemented change, and actual system behavior slowly drift apart.**

If that is the problem, then organizations need to know at least three things:

* what is true now
* what is separating over time
* what that separation or change may affect next

That is the simplest way to understand Proof, Drift, and Impact.

---

## 1. Proof — What Is True Now

Proof is about showing what can be trusted now.

In plain language, Proof helps answer questions like:

* what was intended?
* what execution surface was derived?
* what actually ran?
* what evidence was produced?
* what can be shown clearly enough to trust at this point in time?

Proof matters because without it, organizations fall back to:

* assumption
* fragmented visibility
* partial memory
* post-hoc explanation

In the current state of UnifyPlane, Proof is the strongest demonstrated capability.

That is because the current system already shows:

* declared-to-runtime continuity
* evidence-backed runtime closure
* traceability and lineage verification
* reconciliation and provenance checks
* bounded execution-shape verification
* structured authority invocation evidence

So if someone asks where UnifyPlane is strongest today, the most honest answer is:

**Proof is strongest now.**

---

## 2. Drift — What Is Separating Over Time

Drift is about showing where alignment is weakening.

In plain language, Drift helps answer questions like:

* where did intended change and runtime behavior begin to separate?
* where did design and implementation stop moving together?
* where are declared surfaces no longer lining up clearly?
* what is no longer as true as people think it is?

Drift matters because many enterprise problems do not begin with a dramatic failure.
They begin with quiet divergence that spreads over time.

In the current state of UnifyPlane, Drift is already more than a future idea.

There are already real drift-oriented checks and controls such as:

* pre-run traceability validation
* contract and structural inspection
* runtime purity and binding purity checks
* call-graph verification
* lineage conformance
* evidence conformance
* fail-closed runtime boundaries

So if someone asks what UnifyPlane already does besides proof, the next strongest answer is:

**It already detects and reduces several forms of drift.**

Proof shows what is true now.
Drift shows what is separating over time.

---

## 3. Impact — What That Change or Separation May Affect Next

Impact is about consequence.

In plain language, Impact helps answer questions like:

* what else is touched by this?
* where can the effect spread?
* what now needs attention?
* how far might the consequence travel?
* how serious is it?

Impact matters because change rarely remains local for long.
The hardest part is often not the original change itself, but what that change affects downstream.

In the current state of UnifyPlane, Impact is the least mature of the three.

There are already useful foundations for it, including:

* traceability
* lineage
* structural inspection
* change and drift visibility
* bounded execution and authority surfaces

But important ingredients are still early or missing, especially:

* dependency graph intelligence
* cross-repo consequence visibility
* stronger derived intelligence outputs
* clearer CIA-style reasoning

So the honest answer today is:

**Impact is grounded and important, but it is not yet one of the strongest demonstrated current capabilities.**

---

## How the Three Fit Together

The three ideas are easiest to understand in sequence.

### Proof

What is true now?

### Drift

What is moving away from what was supposed to stay aligned?

### Impact

What may that movement or change affect next?

That means they are not separate islands.

They build on each other.

* Proof without Drift becomes static
* Drift without Proof becomes vague
* Impact without Proof and Drift becomes too speculative

A more mature system needs all three.

But they do not mature equally at the same time.

That is exactly what the current UnifyPlane state shows.

The simpler public definition of UnifyPlane is:

**UnifyPlane helps keep intended change, implemented change, and actual system behavior connected, visible, and explainable over time.**

The three ideas map to that very naturally:

* **Proof** helps keep them **connected**
* **Drift** helps make separation **visible**
* **Impact** helps make consequence **explainable**

That is one of the cleanest bridges between the plain-language website narrative and the deeper system model.

## How They Appear Across the Lifecycle

These three should not be understood as one-time outputs only.

They can matter across multiple lifecycle phases.

### During design

* Proof: what has been declared so far?
* Drift: where is intended change already separating from design?
* Impact: what else would this design change affect?

### During development

* Proof: what changed in implementation?
* Drift: where did code, tests, or assumptions separate?
* Impact: what else now needs reassessment?

### During testing and release readiness

* Proof: what is currently validated?
* Drift: what is no longer aligned with what should be tested?
* Impact: what is the likely blast radius if this proceeds?

### During runtime

* Proof: what can be shown about what happened?
* Drift: where is live behavior separating from expectations?
* Impact: what downstream consequence is now visible?

This is important because Proof, Drift, and Impact are not only runtime ideas.
They are lifecycle assessment outputs.

If we stay honest about current maturity, the picture looks like this:

### Proof

Already strong and evidenced now

### Drift

Already meaningful and active now

### Impact

Important, grounded, but still early

That means the current system is already good at:

* showing what is true now

That is the current boundary.

That is both more grounded and more useful.
