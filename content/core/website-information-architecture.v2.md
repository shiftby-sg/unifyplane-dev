# Website Information Architecture

## Purpose

This document defines the publishable structure for `unifyplane.dev`.

The site should present UnifyPlane as an evidence-oriented continuity approach for helping organizations keep change manageable and runtime behavior explainable over time. It should not present UnifyPlane as a generic product, a hype concept, or a broad enterprise transformation promise.

The site should help a reader quickly understand:

* the continuity problem UnifyPlane is focused on
* what UnifyPlane is
* what is already real
* what evidence exists
* where controls, safety, and security fit
* how AI makes the problem more urgent without redefining its core nature
* what should not yet be overstated

The site should move in a disciplined order:

**practical problem recognition → plain-language definition → current readiness → evidence → operational parts → deeper foundations**

That order matters. Readers should first understand the problem and current truth before they are asked to absorb deeper structure, internal vocabulary, or future direction.

UnifyPlane should be understood first as a **continuity, evidence, and drift-reduction approach**. AI, safety, security, and governance-related concerns should appear as important consequence and assurance areas within that same problem, not as replacements for the core identity.

---

## Primary Audience

The site should be optimized first for readers who will get immediate signal from the problem and the response:

* enterprise architecture and architecture-governance readers
* assurance, audit, and control-oriented technical readers
* security assurance and security architecture readers
* senior engineering, platform, and technical-governance readers

These audiences care about continuity across change, runtime behavior, evidence, controls, boundaries, safety, and security. The site should make those concerns legible quickly.

Business leadership, broad governance audiences, and general AI-risk readers still matter, but they are secondary readers for UnifyPlane itself.

---

## Secondary Audience Usage Note

Secondary audiences matter, but they should not dominate the structure, navigation, or first-contact language of the site.

For UnifyPlane, secondary audiences include:

* business leadership readers
* broader governance audiences
* general AI-risk readers
* adjacent readers interested in safety, security, or assurance implications without being part of the primary technical audience

These audiences should be handled in a supporting way.

### 1. Touch the content where relevant

Secondary audience concerns should appear where they are genuinely relevant to the core continuity problem.

Examples include:

* showing why the problem matters for controls, safety, security, and governance
* making clear why AI adoption increases urgency
* highlighting where business confidence, assurance, or accountability depend on continuity and evidence

These concerns should be integrated into the relevant pages, not used to redefine the site’s core identity.

### 2. Use Writing for broader audience reach

Articles or Writing can be used to address secondary audiences more directly where useful.

This is the right place for:

* plain-language explanations for broader readers
* essays connecting continuity to AI risk, governance, safety, or security concerns
* business-facing interpretation grounded in the canonical documents
* audience-specific explanation that would be too distracting at the top layer of the main documentation site

### 3. Do not let secondary audiences dominate the IA

Secondary audiences should not drive:

* primary navigation
* homepage identity
* first-definition language
* top-level page structure
* the core continuity-first framing of the site

The site should remain optimized first for the primary audience. Secondary audiences should be served through selective integration, supporting interpretation, and optional deeper writing.

### Short version

Secondary audiences should be acknowledged, served, and supported, but not allowed to reshape the site’s primary structure or first-contact identity.

## Core Publishing Principles

### 1. Primary audience first

The IA should be optimized first for the readers above. A page is successful only if those readers can quickly see why it matters.

### 2. Problem before abstraction

Readers should meet the practical enterprise problem before they meet framework language, internal doctrine, or future direction.

### 3. Signal in seconds

The homepage and primary navigation should make four things legible almost immediately:

* what problem the site is about
* what kind of thing UnifyPlane is
* whether it is already real or mostly conceptual
* where to go next

### 4. Trust by structure

Trust should come from how the site is organized:

* current readiness is visible
* evidence has its own place
* future direction is separated from current reality
* boundaries are explicit
* claims are kept proportional to maturity

### 5. Evidence before expansion

If a claim can be tied to current evidence, it should be. If it cannot, it should be labeled as emerging or future-facing.

### 6. One page, one job

Each top-level page should have a clear purpose and should not try to own too many roles at once.

### 7. Problem-to-proof flow

The site should feel like a convincing path:

**problem → definition → readiness → evidence → components**

### 8. Continuity is the conceptual center

The IA should explicitly reflect that continuity is the center of the current canon:

* intended change
* what was actually built, configured, and deployed
* how systems behave in production over time

### 9. Stakeholder-critical concerns must be visible

Controls, safety, security, and AI-related concerns should be clearly visible because they are real stakeholder concerns and common entry points into the problem space.

### 10. Core identity must remain continuity-first

Even when AI, safety, and security are highlighted, the site should still define UnifyPlane primarily through continuity, evidence, drift, and runtime explainability rather than through any single concern area alone.

### 11. Components are operational parts only

Components should only contain real operational parts of UnifyPlane, not disciplines, audiences, or enterprise functions.

### 12. Canonical truth must be easy to preserve

The IA should support source-of-truth pages and low duplication so the site remains maintainable as it grows.

### 13. Distinctive, but not doctrinal

The site should feel differentiated without forcing readers into internal language too early.

### 14. Practical before theoretical

When in doubt, prefer real enterprise practices, real delivery environments, real evidence, and real runtime questions over abstract framing.

### 15. Progressive depth

Readers should be able to stop at different levels:

* homepage signal
* definition
* readiness
* evidence
* components
* deeper foundations

### 16. Documentation, not product marketing

The site should feel like a serious, evidence-led documentation site with bounded commercial credibility, not like a product funnel and not like a concept blog.

Recommended top-level navigation:

* Home
* What is UnifyPlane
* Why it matters
* Current Readiness
* Evidence
* Components
* Foundations

Optional later secondary navigation or footer link:

* Writing

### Why this navigation

This keeps the public path simple and legible:

**problem → definition → current truth → evidence → operational reality → deeper understanding**

“Why it matters” is more immediately legible than “Why it exists.”

“Foundations” is preferred over “Concepts” because it is clearer for the primary audience and less abstract on first contact.

“Writing” should be secondary if used at all. The site should not feel blog-led.

* that it is grounded in real implementation and evidence
* that current capability is separated from future direction

4. Current Readiness

* the continuity problem in software-driven systems

This page should inherit from the canonical Problem Statement. It should not reintroduce older or looser framing that weakens the current continuity-first canon.

* Where continuity weakens in practice
* Why enterprise processes often miss it
* Why this appears across different delivery models

A visible boundary layer should exist across the site.

This can appear as:

* explicit notes where maturity is uneven
* reminders that controls, safety, security, and AI-related concerns are important, but do not redefine the core identity of the site

---

## Content Design Rules

### 1. Keep the top layer simple and practice-grounded

Top-level pages should use familiar enterprise and software-delivery language.

### 2. Keep current truth separate from future direction

Do not blend what is real now with what is hoped for later.

### 3. Evidence before expansion

Tie claims to evidence where possible.

### 4. Do not lead with doctrine language

Internal concepts and structure should support the site, not dominate first contact.

### 5. Keep architecture as an audience and problem lens, not a component

Architecture matters strongly, but it should not appear as an operational component unless there is one.

### 6. Keep safety and security visible, but integrated

They should appear as important stake and assurance areas, not as the sole identity.

### 7. Keep AI visible, but as an amplifier

AI should appear where urgency, newer execution environments, and bounded agent or AI-enabled paths matter, but should not replace the continuity-first identity.

### 8. Keep interpretation honest

Where evidence is partial, maturity is uneven, or implementation is early, the site should say so directly.
