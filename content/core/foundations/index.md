## Foundations

This section explains the deeper ideas behind UnifyPlane.

Foundations are not the first place to start.
They are the layer readers should come to after they already understand:

* the continuity problem
* what UnifyPlane is
* what is already real
* what evidence exists
* where current maturity boundaries are

That ordering matters.

UnifyPlane should not be understood first through internal doctrine or abstract framework language. It should be understood first through the practical problem it addresses and the evidence-backed reality it already shows. Foundations exist to deepen that understanding, not replace it. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

---

## Why Foundations Matter

UnifyPlane is not only a set of pages about one product or one runtime.

It is also a way of understanding a recurring enterprise problem:

* intended change weakens from what was actually built, configured, and deployed
* runtime behavior becomes harder to explain clearly
* confidence shifts from evidence to assumption
* drift becomes visible too late
* downstream consequence is understood too late

The Foundations section exists to explain the deeper ideas behind that problem and the ideas behind the current UnifyPlane response.

These pages should help readers understand not only what UnifyPlane currently does, but why the system is shaped this way at all. 

---

## The Current Foundation Priorities

The first foundation pages should stay close to the current canon and current maturity.

### Continuity

The central foundation.

Continuity explains why UnifyPlane is focused on keeping intended change, what was actually built, configured, and deployed, and how systems behave in production more clearly connected over time.

### Proof

Proof explains how current truth can be shown from declared artifacts and real evidence rather than from assumption alone.

### Drift

Drift explains what happens when alignment weakens over time, how separation grows, and why drift should be detected and reduced early.

### Evidence

Evidence explains what helps show what actually happened, not just what was expected or claimed.

### Change

Change explains why change should be understood across the lifecycle, not only as a release event or code merge.

### Impact

Impact explains what a change, drift, or runtime difference may affect next, and why downstream consequence visibility matters.

These six pages are the current first-layer foundation set. They should remain the main conceptual center of the Foundations section.

## How to Read the Foundations

A useful reading path is:

1. Continuity  
2. Proof  
3. Drift  
4. Evidence  
5. Change  
6. Impact  

That sequence is deliberate.

### Continuity first
Because continuity is the conceptual center of the current canon.

### Proof next
Because readers need a way to understand what can be shown clearly now.

### Drift after proof
Because once current truth is clearer, separation over time becomes easier to understand.

### Evidence alongside proof and drift
Because evidence is what helps make both of those explainable.

### Change and Impact later
Because they build on the earlier foundations and point more directly toward lifecycle consequence.

## What Foundations Are Not

The Foundations section should not be treated as:

* a glossary of internal jargon
* a framework catalog
* a replacement for readiness or evidence
* a shortcut around the practical enterprise problem
* a place to overstate future direction

These pages should remain connected to current implementation, current evidence, and current maturity boundaries.

Where a foundation is stronger as a direction than as a current capability, that should be stated clearly.
